Every school year starts the same way: a small team locks themselves in a room with a whiteboard, a stack of spreadsheets, and far too much coffee. Building a timetable by hand is one of the most thankless jobs in school administration — and one of the most error-prone.

AI scheduling changes the shape of that work entirely. Instead of placing every period by hand and praying nothing collides, you describe the rules and let the system do the placing.

## The hidden cost of manual timetabling

A typical secondary school juggles dozens of teachers, hundreds of students, multiple streams, and a finite number of rooms. The number of valid arrangements is astronomical, and the number of *good* ones is vanishingly small.

When you build that by hand, three costs add up fast:

- **Time.** Two to three weeks of senior staff time, every single term.
- **Errors.** A single missed clash can cascade into a dozen downstream conflicts.
- **Rigidity.** Once it's built, nobody wants to touch it — even when it clearly needs fixing.

## What AI scheduling actually does

schedU treats the timetable as a constraint-satisfaction problem. You supply the inputs — teachers, subjects, rooms, sections, and the rules that bind them — and the engine searches for an arrangement that satisfies every hard constraint while optimizing the soft ones.

In practice that means:

1. You enter your school data once.
2. You declare constraints like teacher availability and room types.
3. You click generate, and a complete, conflict-free timetable appears.

Because the schedule is generated rather than hand-placed, regenerating after a change is just as fast as the first run.

## Faster, but also better

Speed is the headline, but quality is the real win. An AI scheduler doesn't get tired on the third day, doesn't lose track of a part-time teacher's Thursday, and doesn't quietly double-book the science lab. Every constraint is checked on every iteration.
